Context

A new product with a massive untapped market

UPC was a leading Hungarian telco providing TV, internet and landline through cable networks — but only in mid-size and large cities like Budapest, Debrecen and Miskolc. For the rest of Hungary, the options were limited: a local provider with a reduced channel lineup, or satellite with no Hungarian channels at all.

UPC Direct was established to fill this gap — offering the same wide channel selection including Hungarian channels, delivered via satellite to any address in the country. In the first 12 months, demand was overwhelming. Customers waited 2–3 weeks for installation. The question wasn't where to sell — it was whether there were enough technicians and receivers to keep up.

Then growth stalled

After roughly a year, acquisition started to slow. The easy wins had been captured. Management asked me to analyse the situation — why was growth declining, and where should the sales team focus next?

Approach — step by step

Step 1 — Put the customers on a map

When UPC operated only 12–15 franchise cities, a simple table of KPIs was enough. But UPC Direct could serve anywhere in Hungary — 3,000+ settlements, sometimes a single house in a field. A table was useless. I needed to visualise the data spatially.

The engineering team used a GIS application called MapInfo. I learned it, exported our customer database from SQL Server, and geocoded every subscriber by settlement. The result was the first customer heatmap management had ever seen. When I presented it, the reaction was immediate — they could see for the first time how customers were distributed across the country, where the density was high and where it dropped off completely.

Step 2 — Map the competition

Not all low-density areas were equally worth targeting. Some had local cable operators — small providers offering limited channels — which made acquisition harder. I collected competitor coverage data through a combination of web scraping their service area pages and asking our field technicians who knew the local landscape. This competitive layer was added to the map, making it immediately clear which areas were genuinely underserved versus just sparse.

Step 3 — Add purchasing power data

Our marketing department had a relationship with a commercial data provider that sold statistical datasets at the city level — average household income, average spending on culture (TV, cinema, theatre), average spending on media. I purchased the relevant subset and joined it to the settlement data. The key metric was average household income — a strong proxy for willingness to pay for a premium satellite subscription.

Step 4 — Score and prioritise

With all four data sources combined — existing customers, competitor coverage, household income, and settlement geography — I built a composite scoring model. The target profile was clear: areas where penetration was below average, household income was above average, and no competitor cable network was present.

Low penetration
Fewer existing subscribers relative to the settlement's population — indicating untapped potential
High income
Above-average household income — stronger willingness to pay for a premium satellite subscription
No cable competitor
No local cable operator present — satellite was the only meaningful TV option available
Geographic clustering
High-scoring settlements grouped together for efficient field sales routing
From analysis to daily tool

A dashboard the sales team used every day

The scoring model became a live MapInfo dashboard. Sales representatives could select a geographic area on the map and instantly see a ranked list of all settlements within it — scored by expansion potential. Rather than working from intuition or revisiting the same areas, the team could plan routes around clusters of high-scoring settlements they hadn't yet fully penetrated.

This turned a one-off analysis into a repeatable daily workflow. Acquisition started to recover. I cannot put an exact number on the uplift after nearly 20 years — but the outcome was clear. It worked.

What made this work
Data before the tools existed
Built in 2007 — years before Power BI, Tableau or Looker. MapInfo and SQL Server were the entire stack. The constraints made the thinking more important than the technology
First heatmap management had seen
Seeing customers on a map for the first time changed how management thought about the business. Visualisation unlocked a conversation that a spreadsheet never could
Combining unlikely data sources
Web scraping competitor sites, purchasing commercial statistical data, merging with internal CRM records — the insight came from the join, not from any single source
Analysis that became operational
The most valuable outcome wasn't the presentation — it was turning the model into a daily planning tool that the sales team actually used in the field
