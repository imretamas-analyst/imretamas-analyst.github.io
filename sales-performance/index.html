The problem

The leaderboard was lying

A sales agent came to me frustrated. She was ranked second on the monthly top-10 leaderboard — measured purely by number of closed deals. But she had been assigned a smaller territory: around 10 buildings with roughly 300 flats in total. She had worked that territory thoroughly, going door to door multiple times, returning to doors where nobody had answered, and significantly improving her penetration rate.

The agent ranked first had more closed deals in absolute terms — but his territory was roughly twice the size. More importantly, he was systematically visiting only the buildings and floors where deals were easy to close, then moving on. Customers would tell us he had promised to come back to finalise a deal — and never did. He had simply moved to an easier building. This is what we called cherry-picking.

The paper system we had couldn't detect any of this. Agents received printed lists, wrote their notes by hand, and returned the completed list when they were done. There was no way to compare performance fairly, no way to detect duplicate coverage, and no way to calculate penetration systematically.

How territory worked before

Printed lists and handwritten notes

The process was entirely manual. A manager would select a set of streets and buildings, print a list of all flats within them, and hand it to a sales agent. The agent would work through the list — knocking on doors, writing outcomes by hand — and return it once finished along with any signed contracts. The manager would then register the contracts in the CRM and issue a new list.

There were no tablets or mobile devices at the time. Everything was paper. The problems this created were significant: no real-time visibility into coverage, no way to calculate penetration accurately, no way to detect if an agent had kept a copy of a list and was working a territory that had since been reassigned to someone else, and no fair basis for comparing performance across agents with different territory sizes.

The solution

A SQL-backed territory management system

The foundation already existed — a SQL Server database containing every street, building and flat where UPC could provide services. This was the coverage universe. I built a system on top of it that digitalised the entire territory assignment and tracking workflow.

Batch territory assignment
Managers could select streets and buildings in bulk and assign the entire batch to a specific sales agent, with a deadline attached. No more manual list printing
Penetration tracking
Once contracts were registered in the CRM, the system automatically calculated penetration — customers as a percentage of total flats — for every assigned territory
Duplicate detection
If an agent returned to a building that had already been reassigned to someone else — using a kept copy of an old list — the system flagged the conflict so managers could investigate
Fair bonus calculation
Monthly incentive bonuses were tied to penetration rates rather than raw deal counts — rewarding thorough work over cherry-picking, and dramatically reducing manual calculation time
True performance reporting
For the first time, managers could compare agents fairly — adjusting for territory size, difficulty and time allocated. The leaderboard reflected real effort, not lucky territory draws
Daily operations visibility
Management had a live view of which territories were active, which had been completed, which were approaching their deadlines and where coverage gaps existed
What this taught me

The best analysis starts with someone feeling something is unfair

This project didn't start with a management request or a strategic initiative. It started with a sales agent who knew intuitively that the measurement system was wrong — and had the clarity to explain exactly why. My job was to listen carefully enough to understand the real problem, then build something that solved it structurally rather than just patching the symptom.

The insight — that raw deal count is a misleading metric without controlling for territory size — is obvious in retrospect. But it took someone close to the work to surface it. Good analysis often works this way: the people doing the job know where the measurement is broken. The analyst's role is to give that knowledge a precise form and build something durable around it.

Metrics shape behaviour
The old leaderboard rewarded cherry-picking because it measured the wrong thing. Changing the metric changed how agents worked — without any new rules or enforcement
Data as fairness infrastructure
The system didn't just improve reporting — it made the workplace fairer. Agents who worked hard were recognised. Agents who gamed the system were visible
Building vs analysing
This was less a one-off analysis and more a product — something the team used every day. Designing it required thinking about workflow, not just data
Operational impact
The system replaced a fragile paper process with something reliable and repeatable — freeing up manager time, reducing errors and giving leadership a view of the business they had never had before
